Project Overview

Designed End-to-End

VEX U is the college-level competition where teams design, build, and program two robots that work in parallel during a match. The 2021-2022 season - the year we won the Innovate Award - I developed a robot end-to-end, which taught me more about mechatronic integration than any class I've taken.

Mechanism Design

4-Bar Linkage + Intake Rollers + Pivoting Tray

A 4-bar linkage elevates the intake-and-deposit assembly to scoring height, maintaining the tray parallel to the ground throughout travel so game pieces can't tip out. Intake rollers mounted on the linkage pull game pieces in; a pivoting tray deposits them in a stack rather than dropping them individually.

Each subsystem - linkage geometry, roller drive ratio, tray pivot - was designed iteratively to balance speed, reliability, and weight within the VEX motor budget.

Drivetrain & Autonomy

Independent Odometry + PID Position Control

The drivetrain uses independent free-spinning tracking wheels for odometry - separated from the powered wheels so they give a clean position signal regardless of slip or contact with other robots.

I wrote parallel PID controllers using the odometry feedback to automatically correct the robot's position whenever a disturbance occurs during autonomous. This kept autonomous routines hitting scoring positions even through collisions and unplanned field events.

Why Independent Odometry?

Powered wheels slip under acceleration and robot contact. Independent tracking wheels give a clean position signal regardless of drive state - necessary for closing PID loops on actual position.

Why PID Correction?

Open-loop autonomous fails when anything unexpected happens - and something always does. Closed-loop PID on odometry made our autonomous survive contact and still reach scoring positions.

Original Flow

Competition Robotics Across Three Seasons

Competition Overview
VEX rewards fast mechanical iteration under real match pressure.

VEX Robotics Competition is built around yearly game objectives where teams design, build, and program robots to score against other teams. VEX U is the university division, with fewer design restrictions and more room for custom fabricated parts and advanced programming.

I competed from sophomore year of high school through junior year at RIT. Most seasons, I led the mechanical design work and helped bridge hardware decisions with the programming team's autonomous strategy.

2021-2022 Season
VEX U robot with lift, intake, deposit tray, and closed-loop positioning.

The university robot combined a four-bar lift, intake rollers, a pivoting deposit tray, and independent odometry wheels. The design earned the Innovate Award at the VEX World Championship and ranked third in qualifications.

This season became the strongest example of full-system design: mechanism packaging, drivetrain constraints, autonomous positioning, and service access all had to work together under match conditions.

2020-2021 Season
A transitional season focused on mechanism packaging and reliability.

The 2020-2021 robot work emphasized compact mechanism layout and fast iteration. These builds are where I refined the habits that carried into VEX U: simplify the load path, make service access obvious, and test mechanisms before the full robot is complete.

2019-2020 Season
Early competition builds built the foundation for later leadership.

The high-school seasons were the foundation: drivetrain layout, structure, lift geometry, driver serviceability, and the discipline of designing around a changing game strategy. They also gave me early experience leading mechanical decisions with a team depending on the outcome.
